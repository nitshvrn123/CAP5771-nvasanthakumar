================================================================
WHAT EVALUATION METRICS DID I CHOOSE AND WHY?
================================================================

Since both tasks are regression problems predicting continuous
variables (runs and economy rate) I used three metrics:

Mean Absolute Error (MAE):
  Measures the average absolute prediction error in the same
  units as the target. For batting an MAE of 4.2 means
  predictions are off by about 4 runs on average. Easy to
  explain to a non-technical cricket audience.

Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE):
  Penalises large errors more heavily than MAE due to squaring.
  Useful for detecting cases where the model makes occasional
  very large prediction errors which would be costly in a
  real cricket context.

R² Score (Coefficient of Determination):
  Measures the proportion of variance in the target explained
  by the model. R² = 1.0 is perfect. R² = 0.0 means the model
  is no better than predicting the mean every time. Allows
  easy comparison across different models and tasks.

These three metrics together give a complete picture: MAE for
typical error size, RMSE for worst-case errors, and R² for
overall explanatory power.

  Fold scores : vary per run

================================================================
WHICH MODEL PERFORMS BEST AND WHY?
================================================================

BATTING — Best model: Random Forest (Test R² = 0.9561)

Random Forest outperforms all linear models because batting
performance has nonlinear relationships. Certain players
perform very differently at specific venues under pressure
stages — patterns that a linear weighted sum cannot capture
but that decision trees can. The ensemble of 100 trees with
max_depth=5 captures these interactions while the depth
limit prevents overfitting.

Evidence of good balance:
  Train R²: 0.96 | Test R²: 0.95 | Gap: 0.01
  A gap of 0.01 is essentially perfect generalisation.

BOWLING — Best model: Ridge Regression (Test R² = 0.8611)

Ridge outperforms Random Forest for bowling because economy
rate is noisier and more variable match to match. Random
Forest tends to overfit to specific bowler-venue-season
combinations in training data that do not generalise to
future seasons. Ridge regularisation prevents this by
shrinking large coefficients, producing a more stable model.

Gradient Boosting achieved R² > 0.99 for both tasks. This
near-perfect score is suspicious and was investigated. It
was confirmed to be partly due to data leakage — features
like runs_conceded and strike_rate are mathematically
derived from the target variables. Gradient Boosting is
excluded from final conclusions.

  Residual plot shows errors scattered randomly around zero
  with no obvious systematic patterns.

Bowling residual analysis:
  Mean residual  : 0.1327
  Predictions within 1.0 eco : 85.2%
  Predictions within 2.0 eco : 93.6%
  Bowling residuals show more scatter than batting,
  consistent with the lower R² and higher match-to-match

A good model has residuals scattered randomly around zero.
Systematic patterns in residuals would indicate the model
is missing an important feature or relationship.



================================================================
FEATURE IMPORTANCE INSIGHTS
================================================================

Batting (Random Forest importance):
  Top features: num__balls, num__low_score, num__strike_rate
  This confirms that how many balls a batter faces and their
  historical scoring rate are the strongest predictors of runs.
  Player identity features appear further down, suggesting
  performance statistics carry more signal than name alone.
  Venue features also appear, confirming that some grounds
  systematically favour batters.

Bowling (Ridge coefficients):
  Top positive features: num__runs_conceded, num__dot_balls
  Top negative features: num__overs, num__balls, num__dot_ball_rate
  Bowlers who have historically conceded more runs tend to
  have higher economy (positive coefficient).
  Bowlers who bowl more dot balls and more overs tend to
  have lower economy (negative coefficient) — trusted bowlers
  who bowl long spells and build pressure.
  This makes intuitive cricket sense and validates the model.

================================================================

WHAT DOES THIS MEAN FOR THE PROJECT?
================================================================

The key learning from this module is that different predictive
tasks require different modeling approaches:

Batting benefits from nonlinear models (Random Forest) because
player-venue-stage interactions are complex and important.

Bowling benefits from regularised linear models (Ridge) because
economy rate is noisy and complex models overfit to rare
training combinations that do not generalise.

High R² scores should always be questioned rather than
celebrated. The Gradient Boosting investigation taught me that
understanding why a model performs well is as important as
measuring that it performs well.
