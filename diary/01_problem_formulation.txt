Week 1

At the beginning of the project, I explored multiple problem ideas involving data science and social media analysis. My initial interest was in understanding how psychological pressure and public reactions might affect individuals. While early ideas considered mental health detection from social media text, I recognized that such approaches raise ethical and interpretive challenges related to diagnosis and scope.

To address these concerns, I refined the project toward studying performance under pressure using observable, non-diagnostic signals. Drawing from my interest in cricket analytics, I reframed the problem to focus on whether external pressure signals—derived from match context and fan reactions—are associated with increased uncertainty and variability in player performance.

The project uses structured cricket match data alongside unstructured fan text from YouTube comments. Machine learning methods are employed to transform these unstructured comments into quantitative pressure indicators, such as sentiment intensity and volume, which can be integrated with historical performance data.

Rather than predicting exact outcomes, the project aims to evaluate how pressure-related features affect the risk profile of a player’s performance. By the end of this phase, I finalized a problem formulation centered on quantifying performance risk under pressure using machine learning and publicly available data, while explicitly avoiding any attempt to diagnose mental health conditions.
