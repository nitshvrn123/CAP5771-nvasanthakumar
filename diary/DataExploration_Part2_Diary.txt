DIARY ENTRY: DATA EXPLORATION — PART II (ISSUE IDENTIFICATION)

This section documents potential data issues identified during exploratory analysis. The goal is to record and understand these issues rather than resolve them at this stage.

### Duplicates and Uniqueness

No exact duplicate records were observed in the batting or bowling datasets.
Although no explicit primary key is enforced in SQLite, player–match records are effectively unique by:
- (match_id, player) for batting
- (match_id, bowler) for bowling

This definition of uniqueness is consistent with the analytical goal of studying per-match performance.

### Missing Values

Missing values were observed primarily in bowling-related metrics such as strike_rate_balls_per_wicket.
These occur when a bowler does not take a wicket in a match and therefore reflect structural characteristics of cricket data rather than data errors.

Missingness in batting metrics was minimal after filtering to relevant match stages.

### Standardization Issues

Match stages were standardized using the `stage_bucket` variable, which categorizes matches into league, playoffs, and final.
After filtering, no inconsistent or unexpected stage labels were observed.
Numeric performance variables were confirmed to have appropriate data types.

### Noise vs Outliers

Extreme performances (e.g., very high run totals or expensive bowling spells) were retained rather than removed.
Because the project focuses on performance risk and variability, such extremes are treated as informative signals rather than noise.

Final-stage matches are relatively rare, which introduces higher uncertainty in estimates for that stage. This imbalance is documented and handled analytically rather than corrected during exploration.

### Open Questions for Stakeholders

- Should abandoned or no-result matches be excluded from performance risk analysis?
- Is the current mapping of qualifiers and eliminators into the playoffs category appropriate?
- Should minimum appearance thresholds be enforced separately for finals due to small sample size?
- Are super-over performances included, and should they be treated differently?
